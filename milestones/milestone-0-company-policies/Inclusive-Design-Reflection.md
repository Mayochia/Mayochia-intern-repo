# Research & Learn

## Who are considered vulnerable populations, and what challenges might they face in digital spaces?

- Elderly - lower digital literacy and physical/cognitive impairments
- People with Disabilities - visual, hearing, motor, or cognitive impairments who may struggle with inaccessible web design
- Low-income Households - incapability to purchase gadgets and pay for internet access
- Rural/Remote Communities - inadequate, poor-quality internet infrastructure
- Racial and Ethnic Minorities - racism, cyberbullying, systemic biases and language barrier
- Children and Youth - cyberbullying, inappropriate media content, data exploitation
- Migrants and Refugees - language barrier
- Marginalized groups - limited outside contact

## What ethical considerations are important when designing or working with neurodivergent individuals? (Hint: Avoid overwhelming UX, respect sensory needs, ensure clear communication.)

1. Avoid overwhelming users through reducing cognitive load. Too much visual noise can confuse users.
2. Respecting sensory needs like having a sensory-friendly reminders.
3. Always practice giving explicit instructions, asynchronous options, and direct but respectful feedback.
4. Allow customization.
5. Minimize distractions.

## How can you make interactions and content more accessible for people with ADHD or Autism? (e.g., simple language, predictable navigation, reducing cognitive load)

Use clear and direct language. Another is to make things predictable and structured. Open communication, support asynchrounous option, and progress retention are necessary.

## How can we support neurodivergent team members in a professional setting? (Hint: Be clear in communication, respect different working styles, and allow flexibility.)

Practice direct communication. Be respectful with other people's differences in work styles. Implment radical flexibility. Lastly, it is to provide structured feedback. It will help motivate neurodivergent team members and encourage them even more.
